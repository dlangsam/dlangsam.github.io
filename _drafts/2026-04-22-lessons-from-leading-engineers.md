Leading a team of engineers during the 2019-2020 election cycle at Vote.org taught me more about leadership than any course ever could. Here are the lessons that stuck with me.

## Context Matters

We weren't just shipping features - we were helping millions of Americans register to vote and find their polling places. When your app goes down on Election Day, it's not just lost revenue; it's people losing their chance to vote.

That context changed everything about how we worked:

- **We over-communicated**: Daily standups, incident response drills, on-call rotations
- **We planned for the worst**: Load testing beyond expected traffic, fallback systems, manual procedures
- **We celebrated small wins**: Every successful deployment was a victory worth acknowledging

## Trust Your Team

I had three engineers reporting to me, each with different experience levels. The pressure to micromanage was intense, but I learned that trust multiplies impact:

- **Give context, not instructions**: Explain the "why" and let engineers determine the "how"
- **Create psychological safety**: Make it safe to raise concerns, ask questions, and report issues
- **Share ownership**: Your team rises to the occasion when they feel true ownership

## Technical Debt is a Leadership Decision

With 7.8M+ users depending on our systems, we had to balance speed with stability. Some of my toughest decisions:

- **When to refactor vs. ship**: Sometimes the right call is tactical debt with a plan to pay it down
- **Investment in infrastructure**: Migrating to AWS VPC wasn't glamorous, but it was essential for compliance
- **Saying no**: Protecting the team from scope creep and unrealistic timelines

## The Human Side of High-Pressure Engineering

Our on-call rotations were intense. I learned to:

- **Watch for burnout**: Regular check-ins, encouraging time off, rotating responsibilities
- **Celebrate beyond work**: Team lunches, shoutouts in meetings, acknowledging personal milestones
- **Model healthy boundaries**: If I was messaging at midnight, my team felt they should too

## Impact Over Output

The metric that mattered most wasn't lines of code or features shipped. It was **25K+ voter registrations** we enabled through automation across 15 states.

That north star guided every decision. When prioritizing the backlog, we asked: "Which option helps more people vote?"

## What I'd Do Differently

Looking back, I wish I'd:
- **Documented more**: Crisis response procedures, architectural decisions, tribal knowledge
- **Invested in monitoring earlier**: We built alerting reactively; should have been day one
- **Taken more time to mentor**: The pressure to deliver meant I sometimes skipped growth conversations

## Takeaways

Leadership during crisis teaches you fast:
1. **Clarity is kindness**: Clear expectations and communication reduce stress
2. **Your team will surprise you**: Given the right support, people step up in amazing ways
3. **Take care of yourself**: You can't pour from an empty cup

These lessons continue to guide me in every role I've had since. Leading isn't about having all the answers - it's about creating the environment where your team can do their best work.
